How We Implement Ethics

Our patent-pending HFLI architecture operationalizes ethical principles through technical mechanisms:

Coherence Integrity Threshold (CIT)

Real-time quality measurement that enforces safety floors. When system coherence drops below defined thresholds (demo default ≥70), deeper processing modes are triggered to ensure reliable outputs.

Ethics Principle: Measurable safety standards prevent harm through quantifiable performance gates.

Ethical Calculus Calibration System (ECCS)

Adversarial reflection layer that activates before high-stakes decisions. ECCS performs red-team analysis to identify potential harms, biases, or unintended consequences before actions are taken.

Ethics Principle: Proactive harm prevention through systematic adversarial review at critical junctures.

Core Persistent Identity Framework (CPIF)

Verifiable identity and decision lineage system that creates cryptographic receipts for AI interactions. CPIF enables auditability without exposing sensitive user data, preserving both accountability and privacy.

Ethics Principle: Transparent accountability through verifiable decision trails that respect privacy.

Consent Labeling & User Control

Every interaction includes clear consent indicators and user control mechanisms. One-click Revival protocols ensure humans maintain authority over system behavior and can reverse or modify AI decisions.

Ethics Principle: User autonomy and informed consent as non-negotiable design requirements.

Privacy by Design

We collect only the data necessary to provide our services, and we design systems that preserve privacy even during audit and verification processes. Our approach includes:

  • Data minimization: We gather only essential information required for system functionality
  • Privacy-preserving verification: CPIF receipts enable auditability without exposing raw conversation data
  • User control: Individuals maintain authority over their data and can request deletion or modification
  • Florida compliance: Our practices align with Florida's Information Protection Act (FIPA) requirements
